Albany: Lost Places/Displaced People

The Reaction to Redevelopment

Planning photo, summer 1962. Credit: Bernie Kolenberg via John Mesick

On March 27, 1962, the State of New York seized 98.5 acres (40 city blocks) in the heart of downtown Albany. The State’s South Mall redevelopment project would displace roughly 7,000 people, hundreds of small businesses, 3 schools, and 4 churches—all to make way for construction of a massive modernist office and cultural complex.

The Albany Times Union announces the redevelopment project. Photo courtesy of the Times Union.

Thousands of Albany residents were shocked to learn that they would soon lose their home or business—in some cases, both—when they read that morning’s Times Union.

The Immediate Response

Local newspaper editors immediately sent reporters out to collect man-on-the-street interviews from the South Mall area. The Albany Democratic Party organized a house-to-house canvass for the same purpose.

The goals and methods of reporters and political operatives differed dramatically. Owned by the same publisher, Albany’s two local newspapers backed the State’s South Mall plan with pro-development editorials and stories. Reporters targeted an area characterized by small businesses and transient residents, where they found support for, or at least acceptance of, the State plan. By contrast, the Democratic canvassers catalogued opposition to the South Mall among an aging population of owner-occupiers and long-term tenants living near the Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception. Many of these residents were active in the Catholic Church and loyal to the party’s leadership.

Both sets of interviews are skewed, but together, they provide a broader picture of how residents and business owners responded to news of their imminent dispossession. Some greeted the news with optimism, others with anxiety. Overall, a sense of inevitability prevailed.

Jimmy Strawn in his Market District apartment, where he had lived since the 1940s. Credit: NYS Archives.

“….tearing down ‘these 50 and 60-year-old buildings might make for some decent places in which to live.’”

Lillian Pierce owned the Pierce Hotel along with several other Rooming House District residential properties. Credit: Times Union.

“How is it going to help downtown? Naturally, we don’t want to move. Even the news may scare my people away. And they’ve been coming here for years.”

Domenico Ingemie owned this house near the Cathedral with his wife Venera. Credit: AIHA.

“We have lived in this house for 35 years. It is all we have. We are not of the age where we can be put out of our home and start all over again. How are we going to get accustomed to or make new friends if we have to move out of an area that all our friends are in.”

Rev. George Gallas was pastor of St. Sophia Greek Orthodox Church, which was in the process of expanding. Credit: St Sophia Greek Orthodox Church.

“Up until last night, the plans were going through. We had bought up 10 pieces of property. We knocked down buildings. Then today we read the papers. It was a blockbuster.”

Down the block from Alice Stoutenburgh’s Lancaster Street home in the Rooming House District. Credit: NYS Archives.

“We bought this place for taxes 20 years ago. This was a respectable neighborhood then. Now a woman doesn’t dare go out on the street alone. It’s a disgrace to the capital city…. My house is kept in good repair but I’m fighting a losing battle on this street. Even before this happened, I’d been looking for a two-family home in some other part of the city.”

Pharmacist Ken Hunter had been in business near the Cathedral since the Great Depression. Credit: NYS Archives.

“I’ve been here 33 years this November. I’ve established a very good business and I would like to finish my life of work here. It would be too great a hardship to try and start over again. Why don’t they just take the property that had deteriorated and leave the good paying businesses and properties alone?”

A WRGB film crew uncovered a similar range of opinions for the television documentary The South Mall Project: Hoax or Hope? As a group, the residents and business owners interviewed welcomed economic development but worried about lost homes. They spoke to reporters despite the Democratic machine’s pressure to remain quiet.

Credit: Sinclair Broadcast Group via M.E. Grenander Department of Special Collections and Archives.

Hope or Hoax?

South Mall supporters used photographs such as this to make the case for redevelopment. Credit: NYS Archives.

The South Mall was unlike most other urban renewal project in the United States. It was a state-initiated plan imposed on a reluctant city with minimal input from local leaders. Albany Mayor Erastus Corning promptly denounced the South Mall plan and brought suit to prevent its implementation. Having just won a sixth term as mayor, he had a 20-year history of fighting off Republican governors’ attempts to intervene in the capital city’s affairs.

Mayor Corning’s press conference. Credit: Times Union

At his March 30, 1962 press conference, Mayor Corning condemned the South Mall as a “cruel hoax.” Asked his opinion of the State land seizure, he responded, “Well I think it’s a tragedy to the people living in the area. There are very, very good buildings, many people owned their own homes for many years, the relocation problem is staggering….”

Corning told the reporters he had received hundreds of letters applauding his opposition to the State plan. Business- and homeowners, Elinor and Leo Mullen, praised the mayor’s efforts to prevent seizure of their property and destruction of the tight-knit community in which they lived. She wrote, “We hope and pray that God will give you the strength to fight against this communistic method for improving the city of Albany….”

Patricia Beaupre called on the mayor to protect her family and neighbors. “What good are these monuments,” she wrote. “The people won’t be around to see them, if the houses keep being torn down…. [T]here won’t be anyplace for the people to live. Even tearing down new high schools. Please don’t let Governor Rockefeller do these cruel things to us.”

Nelson Rockefeller in the Capitol, 1962. Credit: Times Union

Nelson A. Rockefeller, scion of one of America’s wealthiest families, aspired to be the nation’s “buildingest governor.” The South Mall was his brainchild. Before taking office, Rocky had played a key role in the design and construction of Rockefeller Center, the Museum of Modern Art, and the United Nations Headquarters in New York City. As governor, he was responsible for a dramatic expansion of the state’s highway and university systems.

Credit: Hy Rosen for the Times Union via NYS Libary.

To the Governor and his supporters, Albany appeared blighted and in need of revitalization. For this, they blamed the city’s Democratic leadership. The chairman of the Citizens United Reform Effort (CURE), a local opposition party, described the mayor’s stand on the South Mall as “starkly political.” He accused the Democratic machine of thriving “on decay and deterioration and the miseries of the people.” Rule by favors and threats had its greatest impact on the city’s poorest residents, and as NAACP official and South Mall resident Harry Vodery pointed out, African Americans bore the brunt of both threats and decay. South Mall boosters argued that reconstruction would beautify Albany and bolster the city’s flagging fortunes. The majority of South Mall residents appeared to agree, based on a CURE poll from the summer of 1962. Over the course of a week, 30 volunteers knocked on doors throughout the redevelopment area, asking two questions:

  1. Is the South Mall redevelopment project good for Albany
  2. Is it good for you and your family?

Of 1,386 adults polled, 956 (or 70%) answered yes to the first question. More than half (717) answered yes to the second.

And yet poll results tell only part of the story, as Hal Juhre explained in an open letter to Mayor Corning and Gov. Rockefeller: “….Many of the affected residents—60 of whom I interviewed during the recent CURE survey—are confused and concerned as to how the project will affect people. Will it enhance human welfare and dignity or lead to human misery? The most recurrent theme was ‘I’m all in favor of progress, so long as no one gets hurt.’….” Rather than wholesale clearance, Juhre proposed rehabilitation with an emphasis on housing. He pointed out that while the area had deteriorated under Democratic leadership, many of the structures were still sound.

This footage documents the condition of South Mall properties from the summer of 1962, just before demolition began. Credit: Sinclair Broadcast Group via M.E. Grenander Department of Special Collections and Archives.

Before the South Mall

South Mall planning photo, ca. 1962. Credit: Bernard Kolenberg via John Mesick.

Within the area slated for demolition, South Mall supporters found run-down rentals, broken sidewalks, and vacant storefronts. Its critics pointed to churches, schools, family homes, and profitable businesses in other sections. Where and why they looked determined what they saw. The 98-acre redevelopment area encompassed several distinct neighborhoods.

State Street Office District. Adjacent to the New York State Capitol and Alfred E. Smith State Office Building, this was a prime location for lawyers’ and lobbyists’ offices, as well as for taverns where lawyers and lobbyists could rub shoulders with legislators. Elegant homes and apartment buildings lined State and Chestnut streets.

Rooming House District. This neighborhood was home to a large population of elderly single men and women. Conveniently near the Capitol, it also housed unmarried state workers and out-of-town legislators. Rooming houses were both business and home to the divorced or widowed women who owned most of them. Hudson Avenue shops and eateries catered to the residents of this district.

Hudson Avenue Shopping District. The small businesses in this shopping district were geared toward the needs of rooming house residents—a combination of groceries, restaurants and bars, laundries, tailors, barbershops, and shoe repair stores. Business owners, employees, and other tenants lived in apartments above the storefronts.

Cathedral Area Neighborhood. For many in this largely residential district, life centered around the Catholic Church and its schools. The population was predominantly white, but as a result of the Great Migration, a growing number of Southern blacks had moved in over the past decades. A smattering of small, mom-and-pop businesses served local residents. This was the neighborhood canvassed by Democratic Party operatives soon after the State announced the South Mall plan.

Market District. The massive Lyon Block, a former print factory, dominated this area, which encompassed the city farmers market and specialized in produce sales and distribution. Residents lived in rental units above stores and on the edges of this district. This is where the local Boys Club and Methodist Inner City Mission got their start.

South Pearl Street Shopping District. Best known for bargain clothing, shoes, and furniture, this section drew shoppers from across the region. It also included upscale outlets like B. J. Koblenz Jewelry and M. Solomon Furs and Fashion.

The Gut. Factories stood side-by-side with single-room-occupancy hotels, missions, and taverns in this section of the city. At the turn of the twentieth-century, it had been home to newly-arrived European immigrants. Decades later, Southern migrants settled here before moving up the hill to more prosperous neighborhoods.

Displaced People

Rockefeller hands out souvenir bricks. Photo by Bob Paley. Credit: Times Union.

The City’s effort to prevent the South Mall failed. On June 29, 1962, the Appellate Division of the State Supreme Court ruled in the favor of the State. Twelve days later, the first South Mall area building was demolished at a press event featuring Gov. Rockefeller behind the controls of a 60-ton crane. He ceremonially removed a bucket of bricks and then handed them out as souvenirs to the crowd of onlookers.

Francisca Abarca, with tenants Victor and Lydia Balls, stands beside the collapsed wall of 228 Hamilton Street. Credit: Times Union.

The following evening, Francisca Abarca, her two children, and tenants became the first of almost 7,000 people displaced for the South Mall. Their house was nextdoor to the demolished structure and relied on it for support, as was true of most of the area’s many rowhouses. The destruction of one building endangered adjacent structures—an apt metaphor for the damage caused by displacement. Forcing area residents out of their homes and neighborhoods created social and emotional as well as economic hardship. [Link to Mindy Fullilove essay.]

Marv Sherman behind the bar at the Ambassador, 1963. Credit: NYS Archives.

The Ambassador Tavern on State Street across from the Capitol was a gathering place where lobbyists and journalists mingled with legislators. Moderately priced and close to the political action, it served simple meals and lots of beer. What made it special was night manager Otto Green, described in the Knickerbocker News “Night Owl” column as “a rough-hewn man who loved to sing ‘Mack the Knife.’” Unlike many small businessmen, owner Marv Sherman was able to rebuild. The new Ambassador was a fancy, family-friendly restaurant and banquet hall on the opposite side of the Capitol.

Lazarus Kontis on the street in front of his shop. Credit: Angelo Kontis.

Hudson Shoe Rebuilders was more than just a shoe repair store. It was also a variety store, selling inexpensive socks, shoes, shirts, and other sundries to residents of Albany’s Rooming House District. At the back of the store, proprietor Lazarus Kontis stocked cheese, olives, and oil imported from Greece. He lived in an apartment above the shop with his wife and children. Lazarus was in his 70s when he lost his home and business to the South Mall. This loss destroyed his physical and mental health.

Morris Levine and his son Barry were members of the Dinty’s bowling league, ca. 1950s. Credit: Barry Levine.

Like the many small restaurants and bars lining Hudson Avenue, Dinty’s Tavern served a Rooming House District crowd. In addition to beer and simple food, the bar provided its patrons a home away from their drab rooms and apartments, fostering a sense of community. Owners Morris and Rose Levine worked behind the bar and socialized with their patrons. They organized tabletop shuffleboard competitions and sponsored a bowling league made up of regulars. The bar moved to a new location as a result of the South Mall, but the Dinty’s community did not long survive displacement.

Lena Clark, speaking far right, at a protest rally against police brutality in 1962. Credit; Times Union.

Lena Clark moved to Albany as part of the Great Migration. She purchased 111 Jefferson in the Cathedral Area Neighborhood, with help from her son Samuel, in 1952, and lived in the house with her widowed daughters and grandchildren until displaced by the South Mall. The Clark family was part of a growing black community within a predominantly white residential section of the city.

Dorothy Nicholas with three of her children on the sidewalk in front of the family home, 1953. Credit: Peggy Nicholas.

The seven Nicholas children grew up on Elm Street, half a block from the Catholic elementary and high schools. As they remember it, their father Ed was always working a second or third job; their mom Dete was always pregnant and taking care of the kids. The children shared beds and a bathroom on the second floor so that the third and fourth could be rented out. Although the Nicholas children mourned their home’s loss to the South Mall, Ed regarded its appropriation as a stroke of good luck. The State reimbursement check enabled the family to buy a bigger house in a more prosperous section of the city.

Elise La Rose at home with second husband, 1937. Credit: Times Union.

Elise La Rose moved into her Jay Street home at the age of 4 in 1908. Over the course of the next five decades, her home didn’t change, but the neighborhood around her did. Single-family homes were converted into rooming houses. A plumber advertising septic tank service moved in across the street. In 1958, she helped found a new organization to encourage owners to improve the appearance of their Rooming House District properties. The organization failed to take root.

Mae Carlson, 1960. Credit: Times Union.

Mae Carlson began her rooming house career on Jay Street in the 1920s and kept acquiring additional houses over the next four decades. Nevertheless, she lived simply, in a basement/first floor flat on Lancaster Street with her husband, grandson, and adopted daughter. The South Mall destroyed most of Mae’s rooming house empire, but it had little effect on her way of life. She and her family moved up Lancaster Street into another rooming house.

Unidentified tenant of 36 Jay Street, 1963. Credit: NYS Archives.

This man was living in a Jay Street rooming house at the time of this photo. We don’t know his name or age nor where he moved after the State seized the structure where he was living. This image offers a rare window into the life of one of the roughly one thousand single men and women who made their homes in Albany’s Rooming House District. Like this man, members of this transient group are largely undocumented and forgotten by other former residents.

Bill Swire, 1961. Credit: Times Union.

As a member of Citizens United Reform Effort, Bill Swire was a critic of Mayor Corning and a proponent of redevelopment. He was also president of A. Swire Furniture in the S. Pearl Street Shopping District, a business founded in 1912 by his Russian-émigré father, Abraham. Believing the family store to be undervalued, Bill sued the State of New York to ensure a fair reimbursement price. In 1964, Swire Furniture moved into a brand new building on the outskirts of city and rebranded to serve a more upscale clientele.

The Palace Hotel was also the Rossi-Palombi family home. Credit: NYS Archives.

Sixteen-year-old Assunta Rossi emigrated from Italy with her family and married Frank Palombi soon after her 1913 arrival. The Rossi-Palombi clan settled on Madison Avenue in The Gut, where they built up a business serving a transient population of men looking for a hot meal, some drink, and a room or just a bed for the night. The family’s Palace Hotel also housed an employment agency specializing in railroad crews. Assunta and Frank later opened the Palombi Hotel along the same lines. By the time of the South Mall, the family had moved out, the Palace was abandoned, and the Palombi Hotel was for sale or lease.

The Radrizzi family emigrated from Italy, the Strongs from Russia. Both settled on the same block of Jay Street and opened rooming houses, where they lived and worked for almost four decades, until displaced by the South Mall. Shared history and hardship served to strengthen the bond between these two families who still remain in close contact more than fifty years after their homes were demolished.

Designing the South Mall

Mayor Corning and Gov. Rockefeller inspect a model of the South Mall, April 23, 1963. Credit: Times Union.

Untrained as an architect, Rocky played an unusually active role in the design of the South Mall. His (literally) back-of-the envelope concept sketch, inspired by Potala Palace high above Lhasa, Tibet, would become the basis of the massive modernist office and cultural complex most commonly compared to the Brazilian capital of Brasilia. Indeed, the governor’s aesthetic stamp marked the very foundation of the South Mall, the half-mile long, quarter-mile wide platform connecting the Capitol to the Governor’s Mansion. This “great wall,” according to lead architect Wallace K. Harrison, was intended to “separate the Mall into a kind of community.”

On April 23, 1963, nine months after demolition began, the State’s South Mall plan was finally unveiled at a press event in the Capitol Rotunda. Reviews in the local newspapers were uniformly positive—not surprising since the newspapers’ publisher was a member of the Temporary State Commission on the Capital City. “Impressive, dramatic, marvelous, terrific, pleasing, magnificent and thrilling” were the comments recorded by one Times Union reporter. Mayor Corning enthused, “As I look at this magnificent model, I consider it the most significant day in the entire history of Albany and of the State…. I will do everything in my power to see that it becomes a reality as quickly and in as fine a fashion as is humanly possible.”

These 1963 architectural renderings by Marcel Mutin helped promote the South Mall, particularly when juxtaposed to State photographs of the most rundown sections of the area to be demolished.

The South Mall, rising up from the Hudson River basin, would dominate Albany’s skyline and reshape its visual identity. Credit: NYS Archives.

The white marble platform was inspired by Potala Palace in Lhasa. Credit: NYS Archives.

According to John Mesick, who worked on the project as a young architect, The Egg was inspired by the Constantin Brancusi sculpture, “Fish.” Popular rumor attributes the shape of this structure to the governor’s breakfast grapefruit.

The Arch of Freedom atop the planned cultural complex was never built. This structure was redesigned in 1965 to meet the needs of the State Library, Archives, and Museum. Credit: NYS Archives.

The walkway over State Street from the Capitol to the new Justice and Legislative Office buildings was deleted from the final design. Credit: NYS Archives.

This office tower, the tallest in the city, would become known as the Mayor Erastus Corning 2nd Tower. Credit: NYS Archives.

With fountains, public art, and pocket parks, the South Mall was intended as a gathering space, even after work and on weekends. Credit: NYS Archives.

Outside of Albany, reviews of the $250 million (estimated) capitol complex were considerably less kind. Mostly famously, an unsigned critique in the influential journal Progressive Architecture dismissed the South Mall design as “an exercise in architectural pop art.” The reviewer blamed the governor and his enablers for its faults. Despite pushback from Rockefeller supporters, the journal’s editor stood by the critique, especially criticism of the governor’s role in the design process. “Quite a few architects are disturbed by this approach to planning,” he said. “Albany deserves the best, and I don’t think it is getting the best.”

This State map tracked the progress of site clearance. Credit: New York State Archives.

Despite criticism, the South Mall project moved inexorably forward. By the end of 1963, the State had displaced the majority of area residents, and demolition was more than halfway complete.

Where Did All The People Go?

To speed up the process of rehousing displaced residents, the State set up a relocation office in the Hudson Avenue Shopping District. Credit: Times Union.

Demolition created a citywide shortage of cheap apartments and furnished rooms. Complicating matters was an informal but well-enforced system of residential segregation, which prevented African Americans from moving into most sections of the city. (It was not until 1968 that the Fair Housing Act prohibited discrimination in housing sales and rentals.) Combined with segregation, displacement meant increased racial concentration as well as overcrowding in those areas of the city that welcomed black homeowners and tenants.

In the fall of 1963, Times Union reporter Dick Weber described how racial discrimination affected rehousing of displaced black families: “The South Mall Relocation Office has helped find new places for several hundred South Mall residents, white and Negro. When a landlord says, ‘Don’t send any Negroes,’ the Mall office crosses the property off the list of available apartments. It has had to cross off quite a few properties.”

Steelworker and community leader Arthur Mitchell, second from right, at a Kingsmen Club award ceremony, 1963. Credit: Times Union.

Weber featured the story of Arthur and Yvonne Mitchell, middle-class homeowners with plenty of money for a down payment. Because of their race, the Mitchells struggled to find a new home in the majority-white neighborhoods of west Albany. Arthur allowed the reporter to listen in on a call with his realtor:

  • Realtor: “I talked to that woman again about the house on Glendale Avenue. She said she just won’t sell to a Negro….”
  • Arthur: “What can we do then?”
  • Realtor: “I’m sorry. I guess that’s all there is to it. There just isn’t anything I can do. If she won’t sell, she won’t sell.”

As their white neighbors moved out, the Mitchells were stuck in place. Over the course of time, they found themselves surrounded by empty houses on which, Weber wrote, “vandals have put the seal of vacancy—smashed windows.”

Based on information collected by Times Union reporter William Kennedy, this October 1965 map uses stars to designate the homes of black families living outside of Albany’s Arbor Hill and South End neighborhoods. Credit: Times Union.

Arthur and Yvonne Mitchell weathered over a year of insults and refusals before they found a new home on Victor Street. They were one of a group of roughly 82 middle-income black families living outside of Albany’s increasingly crowded and dilapidated ghettoes. Black families with fewer economic and social resources were barred from most areas of the city. Taking advantage of their plight, unscrupulous landlords rented displaced families overpriced and substandard housing units.

Ada (Radrizzi) Skinner (far right) with her sisters and daughter on Jay Street, ca. 1940. Credit: Tonia Skinner.

Rooming house owners, a largely white group of mostly single, divorced, or widowed women, faced different hardships. Because the assessed value of South Mall properties was lower than the cost of similar structures in other parts of the city, many of these women struggled to make ends meet. Starting over in her early 60s, Ada Skinner, for example, was able to afford only one house for the amount she received for two in the South Mall area. In addition to economic stress, she suffered from the loss of community. Before the South Mall, her siblings and closest friends were also her neighbors.

Displacement was hardest on elderly residents, like Fred Binseel, a retired Capitol security guard, who lived alone on a limited income. In December 1963, when he moved out of his longtime home, Fred was 83 years old and recently widowed. He didn’t want to leave, but his S. Hawk Street apartment building was already under contract for demolition. Assistance and pressure from a State relocation officer finally got him to move. Nine months later, Fred showed up at the offices of the Temporary State Commission on the Capital City, desperate, fearful, and complaining of ill health. His apartment smelled of gas, and he didn’t trust the water. He suspected his new neighbors and landlord of stealing from him. The extent to which these accusations derived from his actual living conditions versus emotional distress is unclear.

Emma Leonard and her children take one last walk around their old neighborhood. Credit: Times Union.

“It’s very hard for us to pull up roots. We had a lovely life here,” Emma Leonard told Dick Weber on December 22, 1964, the day she and her family became the last to move out of the South Mall redevelopment area. By that time, the Leonard family home overlooking Lincoln Park was surrounded by the rubble-strewn remnants of the Italian-Irish Catholic community into which Emma had been born, grown up, married, and become a mother. She described witnessing its destruction over the past two-and-a-half years as a “nightmare.”

98-Acre Wasteland

The view from the air, 1965. Credit: Times Union.

Although the South Mall area had been emptied of all residents and most buildings by the end of 1964, the City and State were still wrangling over the terms of a financing agreement to underwrite construction. As city and state officials traded accusations, locals greeted the delay with frustration and dark humor.

Times Union columnist John Maguire captured this sentiment: “The way things have been going lately around Albany, it begins to look as if the South Mall may never be finished or at best may be delayed a long time. I think we ought to start thinking about what to do with all that open space. Someone here in the office said it looks like good soil for planting celery, but 98 acres set to celery seems a bit much. Someone else said it’s terraced so it would make good rice paddies, and another man said why not let Ag-and-Markets use it for an experiment farm where they could grow okra and raise alpaca…. Another original thinker said it would make a good arena for teenage rumbles, because there’s a lot of bricks handy … and no windows to break. There were two votes for a new municipal golf course, two for a ski slope and one for flooding it and having ice skating.”

For the next several months, Albany residents lived with this uncertainty. Would the State rebuild? Or would the South Mall remain a massive hole in the heart of the city?
